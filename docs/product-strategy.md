# Product Strategy — Next Move Planner

## Where to play

The pre-application phase of the Australian property transition: the 2–12 months between "we're thinking about moving" and "we're ready to apply for finance."

This space is contested but poorly served:

| Player | What they offer | Gap |
|---|---|---|
| Banks' borrowing calculators | Generic capacity numbers | No transition logic — they answer "how much can I borrow", not "should I buy or sell first" |
| Property portals (REA, Domain) | Valuations, listings, market data | No financial readiness layer; their incentive is engagement with listings |
| Mortgage brokers | Personalised advice | Customers delay the conversation until they feel "ready" — the exact problem |
| Bridging calculators (incl. Bridgit's) | Loan-size estimates | Assume the customer already understands bridging; answer mechanics, not readiness |

Nobody owns the question: **"Am I ready to make my next move, and in what order?"**

## How to win

1. **Answer the real question.** Readiness, sequencing and risk — not loan size. The planner's unit of value is a decision, not a number.
2. **Be honest enough to be trusted.** The planner tells some users they're *not* ready. That honesty is the moat: a tool that always says yes is a lead form, and customers can smell it. Trust earned here converts later at much higher quality.
3. **Mirror real credit logic.** Scores are built on peak debt, combined-security LVR and end-debt serviceability — the same frame a bridging lender actually uses. Customers arrive at application with expectations that match reality, which protects approval rates and NPS at once.
4. **AI explains; deterministic maths decides.** In a regulated lending context the numbers must be auditable. The AI layer translates positions into plain language and personalised guidance — it never produces the score. This separation is a feature, not a constraint.
5. **Distribute through the broker network.** Bridgit's strategic asset is broker reach (75%+ of the network accredited; white-label with the major aggregators). H3 makes the planner a broker tool — readiness assessments arriving as warm, educated referrals.

## What we deliberately do not do

- No credit checks or stored PII in H1 — anonymity is what makes people honest with the inputs
- No rate comparison — the planner is pre-product-selection by design
- No property search — partner surface, not core
- No "apply now" pressure — a single, soft broker-conversation CTA, earned by the content

## Business model logic

The planner is a top-of-funnel asset, measured like one:

- **Direct:** qualified referral volume into Bridgit's application funnel at lower CAC than paid acquisition
- **Indirect:** brand association with "confidence during transitions" — the exact positioning Bridgit already owns at the loan layer, extended upstream
- **Defensive:** if a portal or major bank builds this first, Bridgit's category-creation advantage in BNSL narrative erodes

## Key strategic risks

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Planner generates awareness but referrals leak to other lenders | Acceptable in H1 (category education grows the BNSL market Bridgit leads); broker handoff with consent in H2 captures intent |
| Regulatory drift toward credit assistance | Keep H1 free of product recommendations tied to applications; legal review gate before H2 personalisation (see risk-assessment.md) |
| Estimates produce misleading confidence | Confidence score communicates uncertainty; copy repeatedly frames outputs as directional; valuation data integration in H2 |
