# Customer Research — Personas & Insights

> Method note: these personas are synthesised from public research on Australian property transitions (CoreLogic, ABS housing mobility data, Bridgit's own published customer stories and commissioned research), broker commentary, and consumer forum analysis (Whirlpool, PropertyChat, Reddit r/AusFinance). In-role, each persona would be validated with 5–8 moderated interviews before being treated as load-bearing. The discovery plan in experiments.md covers this.

## The shared problem

Every persona below faces the same structural dilemma — **the sequencing gap**: buying and selling are two transactions with independent timelines, and the customer carries the risk of the mismatch. What differs is their equity position, risk tolerance, and emotional driver.

---

## 1. The Upsizer — "Sarah & Tom"

**Situation:** Late 30s, two kids, own a 3-bed in a middle-ring suburb worth ~$1.2M with $450k owing. Need a 4-bed near better schools, ~$1.6–1.8M. Both employed, combined ~$240k.

**Trigger:** School enrolment deadlines. The move has a real date attached.

**Anxiety:** "If we sell first we're renting with two kids and two pets while trying to buy in a rising market. If we buy first, can we even afford to hold two mortgages?"

**Today's behaviour:** Saves listings for months. Runs bank borrowing calculators that answer the wrong question. Delays the broker call because "we're not ready yet."

**What ready looks like:** A clear picture of whether their equity bridges the gap, what a slow sale would cost, and a deadline-aligned plan.

**JTBD:** *When the school deadline makes our move real, help me know whether we can buy before we sell, so we move once and the kids start on day one.*

---

## 2. The Downsizer — "Margaret"

**Situation:** 68, widowed, owns a $2.1M family home outright. Wants a $1.1M apartment near her daughter. Income is part pension, part super drawdown — low on paper, asset-rich.

**Trigger:** The house is too much. Garden, stairs, maintenance.

**Anxiety:** "Banks won't lend to someone my age. I don't want strangers through my home while I'm living in it. And I will not move twice."

**Today's behaviour:** Has been "about to downsize" for three years. Paralysed less by money than by sequencing and disruption.

**What ready looks like:** Proof that her equity does the work without traditional serviceability, and a plan where she buys, moves once, then sells an empty, well-presented home.

**JTBD:** *When the family home becomes a burden, help me move into the right next home in one move, without feeling locked out by my age or income.*

---

## 3. The Growing Family — "Priya & Dan"

**Situation:** Early 30s, first baby due in five months. Own a 2-bed unit worth $850k with $580k owing. Need a townhouse/house ~$1.1M. One income about to pause for parental leave.

**Trigger:** A baby. The least movable deadline there is.

**Anxiety:** "Our equity is thin and our income's about to dip. Is this even possible, or are we dreaming?"

**Today's behaviour:** Late-night calculator sessions. Conflicting advice from family. Genuinely doesn't know if the answer is "yes with care" or "not yet" — and no tool will say it plainly.

**What ready looks like:** An honest verdict. If it's "not yet", a concrete path: what to reduce, what to save, when to re-check.

**JTBD:** *When our family outgrows our home faster than our equity grows, tell me honestly whether to stretch now or build position first — and exactly how.*

---

## 4. The Empty Nester couple — "Robert & Lyn"

**Situation:** Late 50s, kids launched. Home worth $1.6M, $150k owing. Want a sea-change to the coast (~$1.3M) plus renovation budget. Both working but eyeing retirement within 10 years.

**Trigger:** Lifestyle. Desirable move, flexible timing.

**Anxiety:** "We have one shot at structuring this before retirement. Get the sequencing wrong and we burn money we'll want later."

**Today's behaviour:** Research-heavy, spreadsheet-comfortable, slow to act. Will compare every option and remember which tool was honest with them.

**What ready looks like:** Strategy comparison — buy-first vs sell-first vs wait — with the retirement-horizon trade-offs made explicit.

**JTBD:** *When we plan the move that sets up our retirement, show me how each sequencing path affects what we keep, so we choose with our eyes open.*

---

## 5. The Self-Employed Homeowner — "Giles"

**Situation:** 45, runs a trade business. Home worth $1.4M, $500k owing. Wants land + build (~$1.7M total). Strong real income, lumpy on paper; two years of complex financials.

**Trigger:** Business is good; family wants space. Window of confidence.

**Anxiety:** "Banks make me feel like a criminal. Every application is a month of paperwork to be told no, or yes-but-slower. I assume I can't bridge."

**Today's behaviour:** Avoids banks reflexively. Over-relies on his accountant, who isn't a credit expert. Most likely of all personas to be *wrongly* pessimistic about his position — equity-strong, documentation-weary.

**What ready looks like:** A tool that works off equity and estimates, not payslips — showing him his asset position is the headline, not his tax return.

**JTBD:** *When my income story is complicated but my equity isn't, show me what my position makes possible before I commit to weeks of paperwork.*

> Bridgit's own published customer stories confirm this segment: "I'm self-employed which is an obstacle that the banks have to take more time processing. Bridgit were accommodating." — Giles, Bridgit customer.

## Cross-persona insights

1. **Readiness is the gate, not rates.** Every persona delays the broker/lender conversation until they privately believe they're viable. The planner's job is to bring that private judgement forward and make it accurate.
2. **"Move once" is universal.** All five personas treat the double-move (sell, rent, buy) as the failure state — emotionally as much as financially. Industry research puts the typical cost above $8,000; the dread is worth more than the dollars.
3. **Honesty is differentiating.** Four of five personas have learned to distrust tools that always conclude "talk to us". A "not yet, and here's why" verdict builds the trust that converts later.
4. **The sale timeline is the core fear.** "What if it doesn't sell?" appeared in every persona's research trail. Scenario modelling (30/60/90 days) addresses the single biggest emotional blocker directly.
5. **Estimates are anchors, with risk.** Customers over-trust online valuations of their own home. The product must teach calibrated doubt without paralysing the user (hence the confidence score and "get two appraisals" as step one of every plan).
